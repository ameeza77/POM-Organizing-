Core Notes

Chapter 6 — Organization Structure & Design

Structure is the formal arrangement of jobs, authority, and communication — how work is divided, grouped, coordinated and controlled. Six basic elements build every structure.

⚙️ Work Specialization

Dividing a job into smaller tasks so each person focuses on one part of the work.

ExampleIn a fast-food outlet: one takes orders, one cooks, one handles drinks, one manages delivery. Each repeats one task, so work flows faster.
Upside — higher efficiency, easier training, skilled repetition.
Downside — boredom, low motivation, loss of meaning if overused.
Deeper analysis
Managers seek balance. Productivity rises with specialization, but too much erodes motivation and creativity. Modern firms blend it with job rotation, teamwork, and empowerment.

🗂️ Departmentalization

Grouping related jobs together after work is divided. Five common logics:

FunctionalBy function — Marketing, Finance, HR, Operations, IT. Strength: shared expertise. Weakness: silos.
ProductBy product line — beverages, snacks, frozen, bakery. Strength: market focus. Weakness: duplicated resources.
CustomerBy customer group — personal, business, corporate, Islamic banking. Strength: tailored service. Weakness: higher cost.
GeographicBy location — KL, Penang, Johor, Sabah. Strength: local response. Weakness: uneven quality.
ProcessBy workflow — registration → diagnosis → treatment → pharmacy → billing. Strength: smooth flow. Weakness: one slow step stalls all.
🎯 You'll sort real examples into these five bins in Activity 1 below.

🔗 Chain of Command

The line of authority from top to bottom — who reports to whom. CEO → GM → Manager → Supervisor → Employees.

AuthorityThe formal right to decide, instruct, and allocate resources.
ResponsibilityThe duty to perform assigned tasks.
AccountabilityBeing answerable for results — explaining performance up the line.
Deeper analysis
Traditional firms keep the chain formal and clear. Modern firms flex it for cross-team collaboration — but some authority must remain, or decision-making collapses into confusion.

👥 Span of Control

How many employees one manager directly supervises.

Narrow (e.g. 5) — closer supervision, better guidance. But: more levels, slower comms, higher cost.
Wide (e.g. 20) — fewer levels, faster comms, lower cost. But: thin supervision, manager overload.
Deeper analysis
No perfect number. It depends on task type, employee skill, manager capability, technology and culture. A standardized, system-monitored call centre runs wide; a complex R&D team needs narrow.
🧮 Activity 2 is a real span-of-control calculator — input headcount and span to see the levels appear.

⚖️ Centralization & Decentralization

Where decision-making authority sits — concentrated at the top, or shared with lower levels.

Centralized — control, consistency, easy coordination. But: slow, less empowering, rigid.
Decentralized — fast, locally responsive, motivating. But: inconsistency risk, harder control.
Deeper analysis
Most modern firms lean decentralized because environments move fast — yet keep finance, legal and brand central. A fast-food chain lets branches set staff schedules, but headquarters owns menu pricing and campaigns.

📋 Formalization

How much the organization runs on written rules, SOPs and job descriptions.

High formalizationBanks, hospitals, universities, airlines, government — mistakes carry serious consequences.
Low formalizationCreative agencies, start-ups, design studios — freedom fuels creativity.
Deeper analysis
Rules give consistency and fairness but can throttle innovation. Too little → confusion; too much → rigidity. The manager's job is to set the right dose.

🏛️ The Bureaucratic Model (Weber)

A traditional design built on hierarchy, rules, specialization and formal authority.

Clear hierarchyDivision of labourFormal rulesImpersonal relationshipsMerit-based careers
Advantages — consistency, fairness, efficiency, accountability. Vital for exam procedures, records, quality assurance.
Disadvantages — red tape, slow decisions, rigidity, frustration. Five signatures for a simple approval.
Deeper analysis
Bureaucracy isn't inherently bad — the test is whether it supports or blocks performance. Essential in aviation, banking, healthcare and QA; corrosive where speed and creativity rule.

🎯 Situational Influences (Contingency View)

There's no one best structure. The right design depends on the situation. Five factors:

StrategyStructure follows strategy — cost focus → centralized/standardized; innovation → flexible/decentralized.
SizeGrowth demands more levels, departments and formal systems.
TechnologyRemote tools, AI and platforms enable flatter, faster structures.
EnvironmentStable → formal & predictable; dynamic → flexible (see: COVID redesigns).
Human ResourcesSkilled, independent staff → wider span & more decentralization.

Chapter 7 — Organization Change & Innovation

A structure is never finished. Organizations must change and innovate to survive shifting markets, technology and expectations.

🔄 The Nature & Types of Change

Change is any significant modification to structure, technology, people, strategy or culture.

StructuralNew job design, departments, reporting lines (functional → team-based).
TechnologicalNew tools, systems, platforms (a new LMS or AI teaching tool).
PeopleNew attitudes, skills, behaviours (training for hybrid work).
StrategicNew direction, market or model (retail shop opens a TikTok Shop).
CulturalNew values and norms (top-down → open and collaborative).
External forces — technology, competition, customers, economy, laws, social trends.
Internal forces — poor performance, dissatisfaction, leadership change, conflict, new goals.

🛡️ Managing Change & Resistance

Employees rarely resist out of laziness — they resist because they're worried, confused, or excluded.

Why they resist — fear of the unknown, fear of losing job/status, lack of understanding, poor communication, comfort with routine.
How to manage it — communicate clearly, involve employees, provide training, give support, phase it gradually, reward positive behaviour.
🧭 In Activity 4 you'll choose the best managerial response to real resistance scenarios.

❄️ Lewin's Change Model

Kurt Lewin's three stages move an organization from old to new:

1 · UnfreezingPrepare people — explain why the old way no longer works. "Manual records cause errors, so we need a digital system."
2 · ChangingImplement the new method — roll out the system, run training.
3 · RefreezingMake it the new normal — update SOPs and performance reviews to lock it in.
Deeper analysis
Managing change needs both the hard side (structure, process, technology) and the soft side (emotions, trust, culture). A brilliant system still fails if people aren't ready.
🧊 Activity 3 — drop each manager action into the correct Lewin stage.

💡 Innovation & the Changing Workplace

Innovation = creating or applying new ideas that add value. Not only invention — also improving what exists.

ProductNew/improved offerings — a café's mobile-order app or healthy menu.
ProcessBetter ways of working — barcode scanning to cut warehouse errors.
Business modelNew value creation/capture — Netflix: DVD rental → streaming.
ServiceBetter customer experience — online account opening, chatbots.
WorkplaceBetter ways to work — flexible work, AI assistants, online training.
Deeper analysis
Innovation and organizing are linked. A rigid, highly centralized structure blocks creativity — people wait for approval. A flexible structure lets ideas move. Buying new tech alone changes nothing; workflow, roles and skills must change too.

The Five Design Forms

Each blueprint suits a different goal, size and complexity. Tap a card to reveal its workings, trade-offs and best fit.

Functional

+
Grouped by specialty
Employees grouped by function — marketing, finance, operations, HR, IT.
Best fit: small–medium firms with limited product lines.
Trade-off: deep efficiency & expertise, but weak cross-department communication and silo thinking.

Divisional

+
Grouped by product / customer / region
Self-contained divisions, each owning a product, customer group or geography.
Best fit: large firms with many products, segments or locations.
Trade-off: sharp market focus and fast response, but duplicated resources and higher cost.

Matrix

+
Dual reporting
Combines functional + project structures — an employee reports to both a functional and a project manager.
Best fit: project-based firms — consulting, engineering, universities, tech.
Trade-off: flexibility & resource sharing, but "two-boss" confusion needing strong communication.

Team-Based

+
Organized around teams
Work built around cross-functional teams instead of fixed departments.
Best fit: firms valuing innovation, collaboration and customer responsiveness.
Trade-off: creativity & fast problem-solving, but possible conflict and slower decisions if roles blur.

Network

+
Core in-house, rest outsourced
Keeps core activities; outsources the rest to partners — a fashion brand designs in-house but outsources manufacturing, delivery and marketing.
Best fit: digital businesses, e-commerce, global supply chains.
Trade-off: low cost & high flexibility, but less control over quality and partner dependence.

Side-by-side decisions

Organizing is a series of trade-offs. Two of the sharpest:

Centralization vs Decentralization

Centralization

  • Decisions made at the top
  • Strong control & consistency
  • Easier coordination
  • Slower, less empowering, rigid
  • Fits stable, standardized work

Decentralization

  • Decisions shared with lower levels
  • Faster, locally responsive
  • More employee motivation
  • Inconsistency & control risk
  • Fits dynamic, fast-moving work

Narrow vs Wide Span of Control

Narrow span

  • One manager → few employees
  • Close supervision & guidance
  • More management levels
  • Slower comms, higher cost
  • Fits complex work (R&D)

Wide span

  • One manager → many employees
  • Fewer levels, lower cost
  • Faster communication
  • Thinner supervision, overload risk
  • Fits standardized work (call centre)

Analysis Task

Choose one real organization you know well — your workplace, a campus department, or a brand like Shopee, AirAsia, Maybank, McDonald's or Grab.

1
Map the structure. Identify its departmentalization type, typical span of control, and where it sits on centralization vs decentralization.
2
Name the design form. Is it functional, divisional, matrix, team-based or network? Justify with evidence.
3
Analyze the fit. Using the contingency view (strategy, size, technology, environment, people), argue whether the structure suits the organization — or where it strains.
4
Recommend a change. Propose one structural or innovation change, then plan it with Lewin's three stages and one resistance-management tactic.
📌 Case to warm up on — FitBite Café: A 3-branch café where everyone reported to the owner. As it grew, roles blurred, stock went haywire, and service quality varied. The owner added branch supervisors, defined kitchen vs service roles, installed digital inventory, and let supervisors make small local calls. Some staff resisted the tech — so she ran training and weekly feedback. Two months on: faster service, fewer stock problems, clearer roles. Which elements of organizing moved? Was it becoming centralized or decentralized?

Glossary

Key terms

Organizing
The management function that arranges people, tasks, authority and resources to achieve organizational goals.
Organization structure
The formal arrangement of jobs, authority and communication — how work is divided, grouped, coordinated and controlled.
Work specialization
Dividing a job into smaller, focused tasks.
Departmentalization
Grouping related jobs by function, product, customer, geography or process.
Chain of command
The unbroken line of authority from top to bottom of an organization.
Authority / Responsibility / Accountability
The right to decide / the duty to perform / being answerable for results.
Span of control
The number of employees one manager directly supervises.
Centralization / Decentralization
Decision authority concentrated at the top / shared with lower levels.
Formalization
The extent to which rules, procedures and job descriptions are written and enforced.
Bureaucracy
A design based on hierarchy, division of labour, formal rules, impersonality and merit (Weber).
Contingency view
The idea that the best structure depends on the organization's situation.
Matrix structure
A design combining functional and project structures, creating dual reporting.
Network structure
A design that keeps core activities in-house and outsources the rest to partners.
Organizational change
A significant modification to structure, technology, people, strategy or culture.
Lewin's change model
A three-stage model of change: unfreezing, changing, refreezing.
Innovation
Creating or applying new ideas, products, services or processes that add value.
